Deeptech Commercialization Path.

A stage-gated framework that sequences a deeptech innovation from lab milestone to first commercial unit — by the actual transitions that matter, not by TRL alone.

The limits of TRL as a commercialization map

TRL describes technology maturity. It does not describe the parallel maturation of customers, capital, supply chain, regulatory standing, and operational capability. A technology can move from TRL 4 to TRL 7 without any progress on those other axes — and arrive at TRL 7 with no path to a paying customer.

This framework maps commercialization as five parallel tracks, each with its own staging logic. Progress requires moving on all five — not just the technical one.

The five tracks

Track 1 — Technical

From validated lab result through engineering scale, pilot, demonstration, and first-of-a-kind commercial. Standard TRL applies here, but with explicit attention to operability, maintainability, and cost-down trajectory — not just performance.

Track 2 — Customer

From identified pain point through letter of intent, paid trial, commercial purchase order, and recurring offtake. Customer maturity is often the binding constraint in deeptech and is rarely tracked with the same rigor as the technology.

Track 3 — Capital

From grant funding through dilutive equity, strategic investment, project debt, and (eventually) operating cash flow. Each transition requires a different investor profile, a different risk story, and a different set of milestones to clear.

Track 4 — Capability

From founding science team through engineering team, commercial team, operations team, and full commercial organization. Most deeptech failures at the scaleup stage are capability failures, not technology failures.

Track 5 — Regulatory & supply chain

From permitting strategy through approvals, supply chain qualification, standards engagement, and (where relevant) certification. This track has long lead times and starting late is often fatal.

The stage gates

Five major stages, each requiring progress on all five tracks before passing the gate.

Stage A — Lab to relevant scale

Technology: validated at a scale where dominant scaling phenomena are observable. Customer: 3+ named potential customers with quantified pain. Capital: grant + seed equity, 18–24 months runway. Capability: founding team + first engineering hire. Regulatory: pathway identified, no fatal flaws.

Stage B — Pilot

Technology: pilot operating at 1/10 to 1/50 commercial scale, instrumented for failure modes. Customer: paid pilot or LOI from at least one named customer. Capital: Series A. Capability: full engineering team, first commercial hire. Regulatory: pilot permits in place, full pathway scoped.

Stage C — Demonstration

Technology: continuous operation at near-commercial scale, with degradation trajectory established. Customer: offtake agreement with one or more customers (conditional on demo). Capital: Series B + strategic. Capability: operations and project teams in place. Regulatory: commercial permits underway, standards engagement active.

Stage D — First commercial unit (FOAK)

Technology: built and commissioned, ramping toward design performance. Customer: long-term offtake contract executed. Capital: project debt + strategic equity + (often) sovereign or policy capital. Capability: full commercial organization. Regulatory: operating permits issued, certifications in progress.

Stage E — Replication and scale

Technology: second and subsequent commercial units, with cost-down curve emerging. Customer: multiple offtake agreements, geographic and segment diversification. Capital: institutional infrastructure debt and equity. Capability: replication playbook, regional teams. Regulatory: standards in place, certifications complete.

How to use it

For founders: use it as a self-diagnostic. Most deeptech companies are unevenly developed across tracks — strong on technology, weak on capability and customer. The framework surfaces the lagging track that's about to become the binding constraint.

For investors: use it to map diligence. Don't just ask about TRL — ask about each track at the current stage and the next.

For program designers: use it to design support that addresses the lagging tracks, not just the visible technical one.
