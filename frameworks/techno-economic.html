Framework · 04 v1.0

Techno-Economic Analysis Method.

A structured approach to building TEAs that survive scrutiny — from process boundary definition through capex, opex, levelized cost, and defensible uncertainty bounds.

Why this framework exists

Most published TEAs are not reproducible. They cite numbers without disclosing the underlying assumptions, mix scopes (gate-to-gate, cradle-to-gate, cradle-to-grave), use inconsistent learning curves, and present point estimates without uncertainty bounds. The result is a literature in which apparently authoritative numbers can't be reconciled.

This framework is the structure I use to build TEAs that another analyst can pick apart, rebuild, and disagree with on specific assumptions rather than the whole result. The objective is not certainty — it is defensibility.

Phase 1 — Scope and boundary

Before any numbers, define:

  • System boundary — what physical and informational flows cross it
  • Functional unit — kg of product, MWh of energy, m³ of water, etc.
  • Reference year — currency, technology cost basis, market context
  • Plant scale and capacity factor — explicit, with the duty cycle that drives them
  • Lifetime and replacement schedule — for every major component, not just the headline asset

The most common scope error is mixing FOAK with NOAK economics in the same model. Decide which one you are modeling, and be explicit about it.

Phase 2 — Process model

Build a mass and energy balance with the same level of detail you would expect from a chemical engineer reviewing it. This includes:

  • Feedstock composition and variability
  • Reaction and separation efficiencies
  • Utility consumption (power, steam, cooling, water, gases)
  • Waste streams and their disposal cost
  • Auxiliary loads and parasitic demand

This is the engineering substrate the economic analysis sits on. If the mass and energy balance is wrong, no amount of financial sophistication will save the result.

Phase 3 — Capex

Build capex bottom-up where possible. For each major equipment item:

  • Source the unit cost (vendor quote, published benchmark, or scaling from analog)
  • Apply the appropriate installation factor (Lang factor or itemized)
  • Add indirect costs (engineering, contingency, owner's costs, financing during construction)
  • Apply FOAK premium where appropriate (typically 30–60% on EPC, 50–100% on contingency)

Disclose installation factors, contingencies, and FOAK premiums separately — never bury them in a single number.

Phase 4 — Opex

Decompose opex into:

  • Variable — feedstock, utilities, consumables, waste disposal (scaling with output)
  • Fixed — labor, maintenance, insurance, property tax, overheads (scaling with capex or capacity)
  • Replacement — periodic major-component replacements amortized appropriately

Maintenance is often a percentage of capex (typically 2–5%) but should be sanity-checked against the specific technology — electrochemical systems with stack replacement, for example, need explicit treatment.

Phase 5 — Levelized cost

Calculate the levelized cost (LCOE, LCOH, LCOW, etc.) with:

  • Explicit discount rate that matches the capital structure
  • Asset lifetime that matches engineering reality (not policy-driven assumption)
  • Annual production based on capacity factor and degradation trajectory
  • End-of-life value or decommissioning cost

Disclose the formula. Disclose the inputs. A levelized cost that cannot be reproduced from the disclosed assumptions is not a defensible number.

Phase 6 — Uncertainty

Single-point estimates are misleading. Always present:

  • Sensitivity — tornado chart showing the impact of each major variable on the result
  • Scenario — discrete scenarios (low, base, high) for the variables that move together
  • Probabilistic — Monte Carlo where the data supports it, with stated input distributions

The output is not a single number but a distribution with named drivers. That distribution is the actual answer.

Phase 7 — Comparative context

A levelized cost without a benchmark is meaningless. Place the result against:

  • The incumbent technology cost at the same scale and geography
  • Adjacent emerging technologies competing for the same use case
  • Published numbers from credible third-party sources, with reconciliation of differences

What this framework produces

A TEA that:

  1. Can be reproduced by another analyst from the disclosed assumptions
  2. Identifies the variables that actually drive the result
  3. Presents uncertainty bounds rather than false precision
  4. Places the result in defensible comparative context
  5. Distinguishes FOAK from NOAK economics explicitly
