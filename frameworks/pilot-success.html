Pilot Success Framework.

A method for designing pilots that produce decision-grade data — and avoiding the all-too-common outcome of an expensive demonstration that proves nothing in particular.

Why most pilots disappoint

I've reviewed dozens of pilot programs across hydrogen, water treatment, materials, and energy storage. A majority produce results that cannot be used to make a Go/No-Go decision for the next scale. Not because the pilot failed technically — usually because it was never designed to answer the actual decision question.

The common failure modes:

  • Pilot scale is too small to capture the dominant scaling phenomena (heat transfer, mixing, residence time).
  • Operating conditions are too benign — clean feedstock, controlled ambient, manual operation.
  • Instrumentation captures performance but not failure modes.
  • Duration is set by budget, not by mechanism — so degradation, fouling, and drift never appear.
  • Success criteria are written after the data is in.

The framework

Step 1 — Define the decision the pilot serves

Every pilot should answer a specific decision question. "Does this work?" is not a decision question. "Should we invest $40M in a 5,000 t/y commercial demonstration, given these named uncertainties?" is. The pilot's design should flow backward from the decision.

Step 2 — Identify the dominant scaling risks

List the technical phenomena that change with scale: surface-to-volume ratio, residence time, mass and heat transfer, pressure drop, catalyst loading, material handling, control loops. Rank them by the magnitude of risk they pose to commercial performance.

Step 3 — Set the minimum viable scale

The pilot scale should be large enough to make the dominant scaling phenomena controllable rather than dominant. As a rule of thumb, this is often 1/10 to 1/50 of commercial scale — not 1/1000. Smaller pilots save money but produce data that doesn't extrapolate.

Step 4 — Design realistic operating envelope

Run the pilot at conditions that match commercial reality:

  • Real or representative feedstock variability
  • Ambient conditions of the eventual site (temperature, humidity, dust)
  • Realistic duty cycles, including startup, shutdown, and trip events
  • Operator skill levels you'll actually have at commercial site

Step 5 — Instrument for failure, not just performance

Performance instrumentation tells you whether the system worked. Failure instrumentation tells you how it would break at commercial scale. Both are essential. Examples: thermal imaging beyond design temperature, vibration monitoring on rotating equipment, corrosion coupons, sampling for degradation products.

Step 6 — Set duration by mechanism

How long does the pilot need to run to expose degradation, fouling, drift, and seasonal effects? This is a technical question, not a budget question. For most chemical and electrochemical processes, the answer is 3,000–8,000 operating hours minimum. Shorter pilots give you a snapshot, not a trajectory.

Step 7 — Pre-commit to success criteria

Before the pilot starts, write the criteria that will trigger Go, No-Go, or Continue decisions. Include quantitative thresholds (yield ≥ X%, availability ≥ Y%, degradation rate ≤ Z%/1000h) and qualitative criteria (no unmanaged safety events, controllable startup). This avoids the most common pathology — interpreting ambiguous data in the direction the team wants.

What this framework produces

A pilot designed against these criteria produces:

  1. A defensible Go/No-Go recommendation for the next scale
  2. Quantified residual uncertainties with named mitigation paths
  3. A calibrated cost and performance basis for the commercial TEA
  4. A list of design changes for the commercial unit

Where this framework breaks

This is a framework for processes where scaling is the central risk. For software-heavy or services-driven innovations, market signal matters more than scaling. For platform technologies with many use cases, you may need a portfolio of small pilots rather than one large one. Adapt accordingly.
